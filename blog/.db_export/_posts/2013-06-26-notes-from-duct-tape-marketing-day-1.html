Define your customer NARROWLY
Who would have a mutual relationship of thinking of themselves as your customer, not a transaction between 2 parties? Who would become an advocate for you?
What would be IDEAL? Who would you LIKE to work with, not just take their money?
Who can you deliver the most value to?

Commit to a VALUE PROPOSITION
Overused phrase, but think about it
All the little things that differentiate you, that customers appreciate
We tend to overlook, assume they're not important, or even hide them to fit a mold we think people are looking for.
What would your customers fill in the blank with?
"I could get it cheaper somewhere else, but I really love that they ______"
(know my name, use organic stuff, they're local, they always explain how to use what I bought...")

Why YOU?
What's your story, unique point of difference, why do you do what you do?
Do you have an innovation that matters?

MARKETING HOURGLASS

Know Like Trust Try Buy Repeat Refer

Select your primary objectives
The most important (and hardest) part is what to leave out.
Make a "Not Todo List"
He uses Workflowy

How to expand your business?
There's a finite number of ways in the abstract sense

  • Charge more
  • Offer more products
  • % leads converted. Let's say you convert at 4%. If you got it to 6%, that's 50% more money! Get excited about small % increases
  • Customers come back more often
  • Increase referrals. Encourage and track them. Actuals & NPS score: "On a scale of 1 to 10, how likely is it that you would recommend us?"
  •     8-10 is a promoter. They should be targets for case study, testimonials
  •     3 or less  - lets get on the phone and make this right. something didn't work
  •     Middle - feedback for what they wanted
  •     Is there a pattern that shows what happened differently among these groups?

 

GROWTH
Stack rank your products/services/customers
Most profitable to less
Find the sweet spot - most bang for your buck, most happy customers, most profitable , least effort.
Some might be losing you money, some might just be distracting from your best business, some might be not-great fits who then become detractors on social and out there in the world.
Just doing this exercise gives you great perspective. Lets you focus energy on what's working. Might require firing clients, dropping products, etc.

No existing customers? Try to get in their shoes, or interview them
Join associations go to meet ups where you believe this ideal customer hangs out. Start asking questions
Internal & internet research too
How do you describe to someone ELSE how to find your ideal client? What's important to them? Imagine handing this problem of finding customers to someone else. Should paint a very clear picture. Might be a picture, a paragraph, a persona/character.
HOW DO YOU FIND THEM?
Sometimes it's buying a list.
What do they read? listen to?
Are they on Facebook? LinkedIn?
What do they search online?
How do they find out information?
ASK THEM
You're not inconveniencing them. If you're actually trying to understand them with the express intent of delivering more value, they will appreciate it.
Sometimes they're busy, hard to find an appointment, etc, but then you get them on the phone and they 'won't shut up'.
Should be ongoing

UNDERSTAND THEM
Timing triggers? (kid going to college, buying a house)
Research first?
Problem or pain?
Gain from finding a solution?
What do they want? THEY DON'T WANT YOUR PRODUCT OR SERVICE

  • Baby photographer? People don't want pictures of their newborn, they want to preserve the memories of their baby, capture that time while they're still little before it's gone.
  • Freelance website creator? People don't want a website, they want more business. So they need to showcase a portfolio, capture leads, update things after you're gone, w/e.
  • Business consultant? People don't want more efficient processes, delegating, etc. They want freedom, they want to take a vacation again.

 

CREATE PERSONAS
Sketch distinct characters who represent each segment of your customer community
This is personal. Give them a name. Get into their family life. Make them real. What's their greatest frustration in the realm you're in?
I'm your friend: How would I spot them for you?
*LITERALLY DO THIS*
Let's get negative - Who don't you want? Why do people buy from competitors? Why do people buy nothing?
Q: If you're B2B, do you target companies or decision maker?
A: The decision maker. That said, there are qualifications to the organizations that fit better, so in a way it's both. But you're calling on Head of X, so it's them. What're they measured by drives how you sell.

VALUE PROPOSITION
What do you do different? What are they not getting from other places?
Don't defer this to the product. Or "we provide good service". How many people provide crappy service? It has to mean something specific.
Go to yours and a competitor's website, copy the first paragraph, black out names/identifying info: Can you identify which is which? Odds are you can't.
Who would care if your business disappeared? Are you using this for positioning?
User research. bring people in:
A couple companies you love?
Here's a computer, search for a company like ours
Describe us in 1 word?
Delve deep. Not just "they do good work". "Tell me a time when we provided good service?"

John points out: people are talking about distribution of content, not content itself.
Photographer shares "why to use a photographer?" "what to wear?"
One person points out that she sees a lot of content for content's sake. It has to drive people through the "marketing hourglass" and be tied to specific business objectives.
Why would someone use this content to find you?

Q: I produce events, does that count as content?
A: Yes

Q: Podcasts? Can you recycle content?
A: Come back tomorrow :) But seriously:
Podcasting: was big 2005ish. Been in a lull since social media. It IS an easy way to create another form of content. Interviews. Things you can't do (well) in writing. It's taking off again. No one cares what it's called, but audio content is taking off again because Apple's podcasting app comes by default.
Recycling: No, but repurposing works. Preso -> video and transcript. Blog post -> submit to local newspapers or other blogs.

Q: Do you do republishing?
A: Yep. Guest posts create back links when other authors publicize their content. He also links to others' stuff.  Great new tools, perspectives, whatever. Includes abstracts in newsletters too.
WHY CONTENT?
Content produces trust
Content educates (ebooks, newsletter, workshops, demo, FAQs, survey data)
Content creates engagement (automatic referrals and reviews, video success stories)

  • Wufoo form for NPS survey. (7-10, send them to testimonial page. Middle group, ask them for feedback, 1-3 it can send you a text message)
  • Video testimonial party - hire some art students or pros, set up a booth. couple glasses of wine in, ask them to go over and talk about their experience.
  • MailVu - site to send people with webcams to. It lets them record video for you, captures their video for you to embed on your site.

A lot of time content production comes down to just being able to get it all done. "Testimonial Party" format compresses time component to get more done.

Suggestions from audience:

  • Online competition for a prize thru any # of online contest providers
  • Photo competition, winner gets one of his trophies
  • Any time he's done this, his customers get super excited "look! I'm on the website!"
  • Overwhelmed? Everyone else is too. Filtering content is valuable as well.
  • Republish abstracts of articles people find interesting from that week.
  • Content as venue for engagement, lead capture, not broadcast. Invite people to subscribe to your newsletter or email list, whatever the next step is. It's how you get people through the "hourglass".
  • All content has a purpose. Some is to educate, be shared, etc. Some has a specific awareness component, lead gen, lead conversion, or customer service target.

 

Q: What format do your customers want?
A: They want it all. (seems suspiciously broad). People want what they want. People who love video explanations don't want to read. People who live in Google Reader don't want a newsletter. Certain markets will lean different ways, but most will want blog, audio, video, and mobile.

Q: What about 1-person businesses? How to find time?
A: The plan makes you much more efficient. I started myself. Maybe 1500 blog posts in, I got help. Once you are efficient, there are incredible inexpensive resources for getting content started and produced. Not $5/article and done type thing. But there ar epeople who can get it started, you make it your own. Key: has to lead to business objective. What's the highest payoff way to create content?

Q: Why give so much away for free?
A: Most of John's customers don't want to know how to do what he does. They want to know that HE knows how to do what he does. Anyone who wants the free ebook to learn how to do it themselves, he wouldn't want to work with them anyway. So he doesn't see it as hurting sales.

IN DEPTH ON THE MARKETING HOURGLASS
Borrows heavily from the "marketing funnel"
Shovel more into the top of the funnel
Idea is to shift some of our attention (and $ sometimes) from lead gen to customer experience
You want processes in place - sometimes even products! - to move people from know/like/trust through try/buy, repeat/refer.
If people are shopping you on price, you haven't done a good job of educating them on value. Price they're willing to pay is just a measure of their perceived value

HOURGLASS ELEMENTS

Map experience
What are all the ways that we come into contact with a customer, what are the ways that we're not, what are the ways that we could? How can we turn this into a positive marketing experience?
Marketing sales service education delivery follow-up finance.

Audit content
are you producing content that transitions into the hourglass?
Map your content to the 7 steps in the hourglass
Awareness, trust, education, customer, conversion

Review revenue
Try doesn't have to be freebie. Could be a $49 evaluation or $19 book that leads into the $400 or $20,000 product/service.
More or less services, New segments or fewer, maintenance, partnering
Don't get distracted with "what else can we sell people??" but most products can be enhanced with a service, vice versa.

Amplify relationships
Could you introduce your strategic partners? Could you give their customers a workshop? Build those relationships.
Joint ventures, "coopetition", co-host a workshop with competitors to attract customers together more than you could apart.
Can you incentivize vendors or suppliers to supply you with customers
Ex: Him and CreativeLIVE. He's borrowing awareness from them, providing them with content.

Create key indicators
Need to track/measure progress. Can model in online funnel conversion tools. Find "clogs" in the flow.

Begin with the referral in mind
Starting there forces you to think about customer experience, orientation, etc.
Reverse engineer: What do you want them to do/think 90 days after purchase -> 45 days -> at purchase -> nurture -> trust -> awareness.

Hourglass can integrate with CRM.
Tag people at what stage you think they are in the hourglass.
Some ways to automate this too (e.g. if they try or buy, that's an easy one)
Drawback is you can get sucked in to these really big ones
Goal should be a simple solution that tracks customers through a process.
Nimble is his favorite CRM for small biz, tracking customers, how often you've been contacting them.

Q: What's the timeframe for when to integrate DTM?
A: I could make a case for a startup using this. At DTM, they use the hourglass every time they do anything. Any new product, campaign, content. If it's serious, they always go back to: where does it fit into the hourglass?

Q: What was the last hourglass that you did personally?
A: "Total Online Presence" is a high-end program they grew, launched back in Jan. Covering this tomorrow, but whole thing was based on

Q: Converting from try to buy?
A: Can do it automatically if CRM supports this. Can manually move people along, say identify a group of "hot prospects". Call them and ask, target a sale to them, etc.

Q: What's the biggest lever? Where to begin?
A: Depends on business and what you're already doing. For the web design guy, he was missing a strong intro process for new customers. Could save time/$$ by "productizing" that on boarding component. Sounded like he went from top to bottom of the hourglass, so that might be a guidelines as well.

He's presented it as if it's linear, because that's the easiest way to present it. But in reality sometimes the steps combine, rearrange, or a customer can come in at many places.

Q: What about competitors signing up for your stuff? Or invalid emails?
A: Double opt-in or send link in email, so if you put in a bogus email, you don't get the resource. Don't worry about competitors; dominate the world instead. Your product service is probably not so unique that you can't talk about it to educate customers. If it really is, don't write about that stuff. There are a lot of ways to cause anxiety, but if you're producing a methodology that really works, it'll be worth it. Customers call him every so often, saying "X is ripping your stuff off". The good guys always win.
